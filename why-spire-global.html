Why Runlayer, why now, for Spire Global

Spire turned space data into a public company on the strength of four distinct business lines and a genuinely novel AI product (AI-S2S). The next chapter — agentic AI woven through Federal, Weather & Climate, Aviation, and Space Services — is a governance and scale problem before it's a modeling problem. Here's how Runlayer maps to it, section by section.

Governance & Control

Close the same gap the 10-K already flagged

Spire's FY2024 10-K disclosed a material weakness in disclosure controls between finance and accounting teams — the kind of cross-team review gap that shows up wherever there's no single, enforced policy layer. It was followed by two NYSE notices for late Form 10-Q filings in 2025, and it's now formally remediated. But the underlying shape of the problem — four GM-led business units (Federal, Weather & Climate, Aviation, Space Services) each moving at their own pace, reporting up to one board and Audit Committee — is structural, not one-time.

As those business units start putting AI agents into production — touching customer data, satellite tasking, or financial reporting workflows — the same risk resurfaces in a new form: who approved this agent's access, what could it touch, and can you prove it to an auditor six months later?

Runlayer's policy-based access control (PBAC) lets Spire define what any agent, in any business unit, is allowed to do — and enforce it centrally, not per-team. Approval workflows route sensitive agent actions to a human before they execute. Audit logs give the Audit Committee and CTO's board-level cybersecurity briefings a ready-made, immutable record — the same kind of evidence that would have made the disclosure-controls remediation faster the first time.

Compliance & Security

Trust infrastructure for a defense-adjacent business

Spire is ISO 27001 certified across its ISMS — corporate IT, satellite command/control, and ground stations — and its 10-K is explicit about ITAR, EAR, and OFAC exposure given the nature of its RF and geospatial data. Its government pipeline keeps deepening: a Missile Defense Agency SHIELD IDIQ award, a Diehl Defence early-warning agreement, and a national-security-focused advisory board addition (a former Vice Chairman of the Joint Chiefs). None of that shows a public FedRAMP authorization or SOC 2 attestation yet.

That's a gap Runlayer is built to close on the AI layer specifically. Our Trust Center publishes our SOC 2 Type II report, security architecture, and data residency posture — the exact artifact a federal security questionnaire asks for. Built-in PII detection and token masking keep sensitive orbital, customer, or export-controlled data from leaking through an agent's context window, and every agent action is logged for audit, not just logged in principle.

Scaling AI Operations

One dashboard for AI-S2S, GPS-RO, and whatever comes next

AI-S2S — a 200-member generative AI ensemble for sub-seasonal-to-seasonal weather forecasting, built with NVIDIA's Omniverse Blueprint for Earth-2 — is proof Spire can out-build the incumbents on AI, not just data collection. That capability will keep expanding: into GNSS-R sea-ice and soil-moisture mapping, RF geolocation, and orbit determination, run by teams spread across the US, UK, Germany, Canada, and Luxembourg, all on one shared AWS platform.

The risk at that scale isn't model quality, it's operational visibility — knowing which agents are deployed where, what they're costing, and where a AWS-dependency risk (already called out in the 10-K) turns into a production incident. Runlayer's analytics dashboard gives Spire's platform and ML leadership (including the ML, Modeling & Data Assimilation team behind AI-S2S) a single view of agent usage, cost, and performance across every business unit and geography. Deployment management makes rolling out a new agent to the Aviation team as controlled and repeatable as it is for Federal.

Developer Productivity

Meet an API-first engineering org where it already lives

Spire already runs three separate public developer portals — Aviation, Weather, and Space Services — plus GitHub sample repos and a public status page. It's actively hiring backend, embedded, firmware, and network engineers across Boulder, Glasgow, Munich, and Cambridge, Ontario. This is a team that ships developer experience as a product, not an afterthought — but there's no public signal yet of MCP or AI coding-agent tooling in that stack.

Runlayer's MCP server catalog gives that engineering org a governed way to expose internal tools (constellation tasking, the Constellation Management Platform, weather data pipelines) to AI agents and IDE copilots, without every business unit hand-rolling its own integration. The plugin ecosystem and IDE integrations mean the same engineers building aviation-docs.spire.com and developers.wx.spire.com can adopt AI-assisted development with the same platform discipline they already apply to their public APIs.
